# History & Mythology
*Where every empire falls, every god files a grievance, and every artifact is technically stolen.*

1. Zeus updated his relationship status to "It's complicated," and the Aegean Sea gained four new islands named after the resulting children.
2. The Roman Empire didn't fall so much as it got acquired by barbarians in a hostile takeover with surprisingly generous severance packages for the senators.
3. Archaeologists found a 4,000-year-old tablet that just says "meeting could have been a papyrus," proving Mesopotamian middle management was already thriving.
4. Odin traded an eye for wisdom, which is why every Norse decision afterward was made with terrible depth perception and total confidence.
5. Cleopatra spoke nine languages, which was mostly so she could reject Roman generals in whichever tongue stung the most.
6. Medieval peasants technically got more vacation days than modern workers, though "vacation" meant standing in mud at a slightly different festival.
7. The Sphinx asked travelers a riddle before eating them, making her history's first pop quiz with a truly aggressive grading policy.
8. Hades ran the only afterlife with a strict no-refunds policy, and Persephone still managed to negotiate a six-month timeshare.
9. The Vikings discovered America, looked around, said "nah," and left, making them history's first one-star Yelp reviewers.
10. Ancient Egyptian embalmers removed the brain through the nose, which explains every decision made by the later dynasties.
11. Napoleon wasn't actually short, but try telling that to the British propaganda department, history's most effective group chat.
12. The Trojans looked at a fifty-foot wooden horse abandoned outside their gates and thought "free horse," which is why we now read terms and conditions.
13. Athena was born fully armored from Zeus's skull, immediately setting an impossible standard for every birth announcement that followed.
14. The Library of Alexandria burned down, and somewhere a scribe muttered "I told you we should back it up on a second continent."
15. Genghis Khan's HR department consisted of one horse and a very clear policy on surrendering promptly.
16. Medieval doctors prescribed leeches for everything, which at least made the copay negotiable in blood.
17. Poseidon created the horse to impress a woman, which remains the most elaborate "check out my car" move in recorded mythology.
18. The Oracle of Delphi gave predictions vague enough to always be right, making her the direct ancestor of every quarterly earnings call.
19. Rome wasn't built in a day, mostly because the contractors kept getting conquered mid-project.
20. Loki turned himself into a mare and gave birth to an eight-legged horse, and Norse family reunions were never boring again.
21. The Aztecs invented mandatory ballgames where the losers were sacrificed, which is still gentler than modern youth sports parents.
22. Anne Boleyn's last words were reportedly gracious, which is impressive given that her performance review ended in a career-limiting way.
23. The Egyptians buried pharaohs with everything they'd need in the afterlife, including servants, which really put "taking work home" in perspective.
24. Hercules completed twelve labors, and his cousin Eurystheus took credit for all of them, inventing the manager.
25. The Hundred Years' War lasted 116 years because medieval Europe couldn't even end a war without a scheduling conflict.
26. Icarus's father gave him exactly one workplace safety briefing, and Icarus treated it the way everyone treats workplace safety briefings.
27. Ancient Sparta made seven-year-olds join the army, which explains why no Spartan ever wrote a tender coming-of-age memoir.
28. Ra sailed the sun across the sky every single day without a single sick day, and Egyptians still complained about the heat.
29. The Rosetta Stone was basically the ancient world leaving itself a password hint, and it still took us two thousand years to log in.
30. Attila the Hun died of a nosebleed on his wedding night, proving the universe has a dark sense of comedic timing.
31. Marie Antoinette never said "let them eat cake," but history decided she needed a catchphrase for the poster.
32. Thor's hammer could only be lifted by the worthy, making it the first piece of equipment with biometric security and a superiority complex.
33. The Neanderthals didn't go extinct so much as they got absorbed into the family and stopped answering the group chat.
34. Julius Caesar was warned about the Ides of March and went to work anyway, becoming the patron saint of ignoring calendar notifications.
35. Medusa's hair was snakes, and yet her stylist still asked if she wanted layers.
36. The pyramids were aligned with the stars, budgeted with slaves' worth of bread and beer, and delivered on time, which no modern project can claim even once.
37. Prometheus gave humans fire and got his liver eaten daily forever, which is why gods stopped doing charity work.
38. The Byzantine Empire spent so much time on palace intrigue that "byzantine" became an insult, the only empire to become its own performance review.
39. William the Conqueror's coronation guards heard cheering, assumed a riot, and set fire to the neighborhood, setting the tone for the entire Norman administration.
40. Anubis weighed your heart against a feather, history's only customs process where the penalty for excess baggage was being eaten by a crocodile hybrid.
41. The Silk Road was just ancient Amazon with a two-year delivery window and a nonzero chance your package got conquered.
42. King Midas turned everything he touched to gold and starved, becoming the first cautionary tale about undiversified assets.
43. The Salem witch trials accepted spectral evidence, meaning dreams were admissible in court, which would make modern litigation extremely interesting.
44. Freya's chariot was pulled by two cats, meaning the Norse goddess of war spent every commute negotiating with animals who did not respect her.
45. Alexander the Great wept when there were no more worlds to conquer, because therapy hadn't been invented and neither had hobbies.
46. Ancient Roman concrete gets stronger with age, unlike ancient Roman marriages, which mostly ended in strategic poisoning.
47. Sisyphus rolls his boulder up the hill forever, which the gods called punishment and gym owners call a subscription model.
48. The Mayans predicted eclipses centuries in advance but somehow didn't see the Spanish coming, proving all forecasting has blind spots.
49. Henry VIII founded a whole new church to get divorced, history's most expensive way to avoid an awkward conversation.
50. The Minotaur lived in a labyrinth built by his stepdad, which is somehow still a healthier arrangement than most Greek myth families.
51. Vikings named Greenland "green" to trick settlers into moving there, inventing real estate marketing a thousand years early.
52. Cerberus had three heads and one job, and souls still occasionally slipped past, proving headcount doesn't equal productivity.
53. The Great Wall of China took two thousand years to build, which means somewhere there was a foreman whose family held the same unfinished ticket for forty generations.
54. Dionysus was the god of wine and theater, meaning the Greeks correctly identified that those two departments should share a budget.
55. The guillotine was marketed as humane and egalitarian, making it the first execution device with a mission statement.
56. Hatshepsut ruled Egypt as pharaoh, and her successor chiseled her name off everything, inventing the quiet delete of an ex's photos.
57. The Mongols were so efficient that Europe's early-warning system was mostly just the smoke from the last town over.
58. Apollo was the god of prophecy, music, healing, and the sun, because Olympus never learned to say no to scope creep.
59. Pompeii's residents were preserved mid-activity by volcanic ash, becoming history's least consensual performance art installation.
60. The Code of Hammurabi carved "an eye for an eye" in stone, because Bronze Age lawyers billed by the chisel stroke.
61. Persephone ate six pomegranate seeds in the underworld and got six months of mandatory winter residency, history's harshest snack tax.
62. Leonardo da Vinci left most of his projects unfinished, comforting procrastinators for five hundred years and counting.
63. The Spanish Armada was defeated partly by weather, and England spent the next four centuries acting like they'd planned the storm.
64. Osiris was murdered, dismembered, reassembled, and promoted to god of the dead, the ancient world's most extreme career pivot.
65. Ancient Greek democracy excluded women, slaves, and foreigners, meaning "rule by the people" came with a very aggressive definition of people.
66. Joan of Arc heard voices telling her to lead armies, and the French, out of options, said "sure, the teenager, why not."
67. The Colossus of Rhodes stood for only 54 years before an earthquake dropped it, wonder of the world with a starter-home lifespan.
68. Norse warriors wanted to die in battle to reach Valhalla, making them the only army in history where the pension plan required dying.
69. The printing press let anyone spread ideas quickly, and within decades Europe was at war over pamphlets, previewing every comment section to come.
70. Aphrodite was born from sea foam, which sounds lovely until you learn which god's severed parts made the foam.
71. Stonehenge took fifteen hundred years to complete, and we still don't know what it's for, making it history's longest-running unlabeled deliverable.
72. Caligula allegedly planned to make his horse a consul, and honestly the Senate had seen worse candidates confirm.
73. The Black Death killed a third of Europe, and the survivors' wages went up, the darkest possible argument for labor scarcity.
74. Hera spent eternity punishing Zeus's affairs by targeting everyone except Zeus, mythology's most misdirected HR investigation.
75. Ancient Olympians competed nude, which certainly simplified the sponsorship deals.
76. The Ottomans besieged Constantinople with a cannon so large it took sixty oxen to move, because compensating has always scaled with empire size.
77. Quetzalcoatl was a feathered serpent god, proof that Mesoamerican theology was designed by a committee that couldn't choose between two mascots.
78. Cato the Elder ended every speech with "Carthage must be destroyed," history's first man to reply-all the same message for twenty years.
79. Medieval knights' armor was so heavy that falling off your horse was less a setback and more a permanent lifestyle change.
80. The Epic of Gilgamesh is humanity's oldest story, and it's about a king who couldn't cope with mortality, so literature has been in beta on the same bug for four thousand years.
